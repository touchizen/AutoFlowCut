# Synopsis Writing Guidelines (Dramatron Method)

## Dramatron Hierarchical Approach

Follow the Dramatron pipeline — each level feeds into the next:

### Step 1: Log Line
Write a 1-2 sentence summary containing:
- **Setting**: Time period and location
- **Protagonist**: Who drives the story
- **Antagonist**: Who opposes them (can be hidden)
- **Conflict/Goal**: What's at stake
- **Inciting Incident**: What triggers the story

Example:
> "In 1888 London, a determined female journalist infiltrates the East End's darkest corners to unmask Jack the Ripper, only to discover the killer may be someone from her own social circle — and that powerful forces will kill to keep the truth buried."

### Step 2: Character Descriptions
For each character, define:
- **Name & Role**: Full name, title/position
- **Physical Description**: Age, appearance, distinguishing features
- **Personality**: Core traits, flaws, desires
- **Secret/Hidden Agenda**: What they're hiding (critical for mystery)
- **Relationship Web**: How they connect to other characters

**Rule**: At least 2 characters must be plausible suspects/sources of mystery.

### Step 3: Plot Points (20-Chapter Framework)

#### Act I — Setup (Chapters 1-5)
| Ch | Purpose | Content |
|----|---------|---------|
| 1 | Hook / Cold Open | Shocking opening scene, question planted |
| 2 | World Introduction | Setting, era, atmosphere established |
| 3 | Protagonist Introduction | Character, motivation, ordinary world |
| 4 | Inciting Incident | The event that changes everything |
| 5 | Call to Adventure | Protagonist commits to the journey |

#### Act II — Rising Action (Chapters 6-12)
| Ch | Purpose | Content |
|----|---------|---------|
| 6 | First Investigation | Initial clues, false leads |
| 7 | Ally/Mentor | Key relationship formed |
| 8 | Deepening Mystery | More suspects, contradictory evidence |
| 9 | First Setback | Protagonist fails or is misled |
| 10 | Midpoint Twist | Major revelation changes perspective |
| 11 | Raising Stakes | Personal danger or emotional cost |
| 12 | Dark Night | Lowest point, all seems lost |

#### Act III — Climax (Chapters 13-17)
| Ch | Purpose | Content |
|----|---------|---------|
| 13 | Renewed Determination | New approach or hidden clue |
| 14 | Closing In | Evidence converges |
| 15 | False Resolution | Wrong answer seems right |
| 16 | True Revelation | The real truth exposed |
| 17 | Climactic Confrontation | Final showdown |

#### Act IV — Resolution (Chapters 18-20)
| Ch | Purpose | Content |
|----|---------|---------|
| 18 | Aftermath | Immediate consequences |
| 19 | New Equilibrium | How the world has changed |
| 20 | Epilogue / Reflection | Final thought, lesson, or lingering question |

### Step 4: Location Descriptions
For each unique location:
- **Name**: Place name
- **Era-Accurate Details**: Architecture, lighting, sounds, smells
- **Atmosphere**: Mood, emotional tone
- **Narrative Function**: Why this location matters to the story

### Step 5: Scene-by-Scene Breakdown
For each chapter:
- **Location**: Where it takes place
- **Characters Present**: Who appears
- **Beat**: What happens (action summary)
- **Emotional Arc**: How tension/mood shifts
- **Foreshadowing**: Seeds planted for later payoff

## Mystery Structure Rules

### Suspect Management
- **Minimum 2 viable suspects** at all times through Act II
- Each suspect must have **means, motive, AND opportunity**
- Red herrings must be **logically constructed** (not arbitrary)

### Information Release Schedule
| Act | Viewer Knowledge Level |
|-----|----------------------|
| Act I | Knows something is wrong, but not what |
| Act II | Has clues but cannot determine the truth |
| Act III (early) | Thinks they know — but they're wrong |
| Act III (late) | Truth revealed with full impact |
| Act IV | Understanding the consequences |

### Foreshadowing Tracker
Create a tracking table:

| Planted In | Clue/Detail | Payoff In | Type |
|-----------|-------------|----------|------|
| Ch 2 | Character mentions a key | Ch 16 | Direct |
| Ch 5 | Background detail in scene | Ch 14 | Subtle |
| Ch 8 | Dialogue with double meaning | Ch 17 | Verbal |

**Rule**: Every Act III revelation must have at least 2 foreshadowing seeds planted in Acts I-II.

## Genre-Specific Guidelines

### Dark History
- Ground in real historical events
- Highlight lesser-known true facts
- "Truth is stranger than fiction" moments
- Modern relevance or parallel

### Medieval Mystery
- Feudal power dynamics drive conflict
- Religion, superstition, and science clash
- Limited forensics = reliance on testimony and deduction

### Tudor/Renaissance Intrigue
- Court politics and succession crises
- Letters, poison, and espionage
- Personal ambition masked as duty

### Gothic Tales
- Atmosphere is a character itself
- Psychological horror over physical
- Unreliable narration heightens mystery

### True Crime History
- Stick to documented facts for core events
- Dramatize gaps in the historical record
- Multiple theories presented fairly
