# Narrative Techniques — Dark History Storytelling

## Voice-Over Narration Styles

### The Historian
Authoritative, measured, like a documentary narrator:
> "In the winter of 1536, something extraordinary happened within the walls of the Tower of London — something that would be deliberately erased from every official record."

### The Storyteller
Warm, engaging, like someone telling a tale by firelight:
> "Now, what you have to understand about Anne Boleyn is this — she was never the villain they made her out to be. But she wasn't exactly innocent, either."

### The Witness
First-person perspective from a fictional observer:
> "I was there that night. I saw the carriage arrive. And I'll tell you now — what they said happened inside that house? That's not even close to the truth."

### Choose ONE style per episode and maintain consistency.

## Scene Construction

### The Five-Beat Scene
Each scene should hit these beats:

1. **Establish**: Where are we? Who's present? What's the mood?
2. **Develop**: New information, interaction, or action
3. **Complicate**: Something unexpected happens
4. **Escalate**: Stakes rise or tension increases
5. **Turn**: Scene ends differently than it began

### Scene Length Guidelines
| Scene Type | Target Duration | Chapters |
|-----------|----------------|----------|
| Action/Confrontation | 45-60 seconds narration | Short |
| Investigation/Discovery | 60-90 seconds | Medium |
| Atmospheric/Setting | 30-45 seconds | Brief |
| Emotional/Dialogue | 60-90 seconds | Medium |
| Revelation/Twist | 45-75 seconds | Varied |

## Tension Building Techniques

### The Slow Reveal
Layer information gradually:
```
Chapter 3: "There was a third person at the dinner that night."
Chapter 7: "The third guest arrived uninvited."
Chapter 11: "The third guest carried something hidden beneath their cloak."
Chapter 16: "The third guest was not who anyone believed them to be."
```

### The False Comfort
Create a moment of safety, then shatter it:
- Characters celebrate a victory → new threat emerges
- Mystery appears solved → contradictory evidence surfaces
- Danger seems past → the real danger was waiting

### Mounting Dread
Use environmental details to build unease:
- Normal scene, but one wrong detail (a locked door, a missing portrait)
- Characters notice something "off" but can't identify it
- Gradually escalating wrongness until the reveal

## Historical Storytelling Techniques

### The "What If" Framework
Present historical facts, then explore:
- "History tells us X happened. But what if Y was the real reason?"
- "The official record says Z. But a recently discovered letter tells a different story."

### Contrast Past and Present
- "Today, this building is a museum. In 1743, it was a prison."
- "We walk past this street every day. Three hundred years ago, it ran red with blood."

### Humanizing Historical Figures
- Give them mundane details (what they ate, feared, loved)
- Show private moments behind public facades
- Present their decisions as choices, not inevitabilities
