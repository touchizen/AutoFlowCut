# Screenplay Writing Guidelines — Dark History

## Script Format

Each chapter follows this structure:

```
## Chapter {N}: {Title}

**[Location — Time]**

(Narration)
{Voice-over narration setting the scene}

(Dialogue — {Character Name})
"{Spoken line}"

(SFX: {sound effect description})

(Narration)
{Continuing narration}
```

---

## Writing Order

**Act I → Act II → Act III → Act IV → Hook**

The Hook (cold open) is written LAST because:
- It must tease the most compelling moment
- You need to know the full story to choose the right hook
- It sets viewer expectations that must be fulfilled

---

## Narration Techniques

### Show, Don't Tell
- BAD: "Lord Harrington was a cruel man who enjoyed others' suffering."
- GOOD: "Lord Harrington watched the eviction from his carriage window. A smile crossed his lips as the family dragged their belongings into the rain."

### Atmospheric Description
Build mood through sensory details:
- **Sight**: Flickering candlelight, fog-choked streets, crumbling stone
- **Sound**: Creaking floorboards, distant church bells, whispered prayers
- **Smell**: Damp earth, burning coal, lavender and decay
- **Touch**: Cold iron, rough parchment, silk hiding steel

### Pacing Control
| Scene Type | Sentence Style | Effect |
|-----------|---------------|--------|
| Tension building | Short. Clipped. Abrupt. | Anxiety |
| Discovery/Revelation | Medium, flowing sentences with pauses | Weight |
| Action/Chase | Rapid-fire, breathless | Urgency |
| Reflection/Aftermath | Long, contemplative, measured | Processing |

## Dialogue Guidelines

### Period-Appropriate Speech
| Era | Style |
|-----|-------|
| Ancient/Classical | Formal, philosophical, declarative |
| Medieval | Feudal hierarchy reflected in address, religious references |
| Tudor/Renaissance | Elaborate courtesy, veiled threats, double meanings |
| Victorian/Gothic | Proper, repressed, loaded with subtext |
| Early Modern | Mix of formal and emerging colloquial |

### Character Voice Differentiation
Each character should be identifiable by speech alone:
- **Noble/Educated**: Complex sentences, Latin phrases, indirect speech
- **Common/Working class**: Direct, practical, idiomatic
- **Military/Authority**: Commands, brevity, chain-of-command language
- **Scholar/Clergy**: Biblical references, philosophical musings
- **Villain (hidden)**: Normal speech with occasional tells

### Subtext
Characters rarely say exactly what they mean:
- A threat delivered as a compliment
- A warning disguised as casual observation
- Love expressed through concern about trivial things
- Guilt revealed through overcompensation

## Suspense Techniques

### The Ticking Clock
Establish a deadline that creates urgency:
- "The trial begins at dawn"
- "The ship sails with the tide"
- "Before the king returns from the hunt"

### Dramatic Irony
Let the viewer know something the protagonist doesn't:
- We see the letter being intercepted
- We know the ally is actually the traitor
- We recognize the poison being prepared

### The Unreliable Witness
Present testimony that seems trustworthy but contains:
- Honest mistakes (misremembering details)
- Deliberate lies (protecting themselves or others)
- Partial truths (technically accurate but misleading)

### Cliffhangers (Chapter Endings)
Each chapter should end with one of:
- **Question**: New mystery introduced
- **Revelation**: Shocking information revealed
- **Danger**: Immediate threat to a character
- **Decision**: Character faces impossible choice

### Red Herring Construction
A good red herring must:
1. Be logically consistent with available evidence
2. Have its own complete narrative logic
3. Be definitively disproven (not just forgotten)
4. Serve the story even after being revealed as false

## Narrative Techniques

### Cold Open (Hook)
Written last. Choose from:
- **Flash-forward**: Show a dramatic moment from Act III
- **Mystery opening**: Present the aftermath of an event
- **Provocative statement**: Narrator makes a bold claim
- **Sensory immersion**: Drop viewer into a vivid scene

### Parallel Timelines
Juxtapose past and present:
- "While the court celebrated, in the dungeon below..."
- "Years earlier, in this same room..."
- "At that very moment, across the city..."

### Motif and Symbolism
Recurring elements that gain meaning:
- A specific object (a ring, a letter, a key)
- A phrase repeated in different contexts
- A location revisited under changed circumstances
- Weather/season mirroring emotional state

### Chapter Transitions
Connect chapters through:
- **Echo**: Last word/image of one chapter reflected in first of next
- **Contrast**: Sharp shift in tone or setting
- **Continuation**: Direct timeline progression
- **Thematic link**: Different scene, same theme

---

## Quality Checklist (Per Chapter)

- [ ] Opens with a hook (question, tension, or intrigue)
- [ ] Advances the plot OR deepens character (preferably both)
- [ ] Contains at least one moment of tension or surprise
- [ ] Ends with forward momentum (why keep watching?)
- [ ] Narration and dialogue are balanced
- [ ] Historical details are accurate and atmospheric
- [ ] No exposition dumps — information woven into action
- [ ] SFX notes are specific and enhance mood
