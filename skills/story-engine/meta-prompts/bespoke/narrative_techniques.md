# Narrative Techniques — Bespoke Genre (Universal)

Genre-neutral narrative-craft toolkit for W3. Use alongside `screenplay_guidelines.md`, `suspense_techniques.md`, and the per-episode `_meta_supplement.md`.

---

## 1. The motif callback

A small detail planted in Act I returns as a transformed image in Act IV.

**Pattern**: throwaway domestic detail in ch.1 (e.g. "the boy ran to fetch eggs") → mythic image in ch.20 ("hands empty of eggs in the field").

The callback works because:
- Act I plants are NOT highlighted — they pass as ambient texture
- Act IV reprise carries the weight of everything between
- The audience feels the resonance without being told to

**How to deploy**: in the synopsis, mark which Act-I detail will become the closing image. In W3, write the Act-I instance flat and matter-of-fact. Reserve the resonance for Act IV.

---

## 2. The withheld detail (Chekhov's bomb)

Plant a fact early. Don't explain why it matters. Pay it off later.

**Examples**:
- "He had not, that day, examined the throat" → ch.8 autopsy reveals fingerprints on throat
- "Andy ran back white-faced" → ch.20 he's still running through snow

The technique:
1. The detail is concrete and visual
2. It's stated without commentary (no "this would matter later" signaling)
3. The payoff makes the original line glow in retrospect

---

## 3. The reversal cascade

When the reveal lands, the prose lists the seeds in order to make the audience feel the inevitability.

**Pattern (one paragraph)**:
> The high collar. The scarf called a favorite. The doctor driven from the body. The pillow on one side and the cloth on the other. The first wife who would not come to Lewisburg. The second wife on Droop Mountain. The blacksmith's hands.

The cascade:
- Each item is a previously-planted seed
- Listed as crystallized images, not explanation
- Audience feels the pattern snap together

This is the highest-impact prose move in the script. Reserve it for ch.16 reveal.

---

## 4. The dramatic irony fold

The audience knows something the characters don't yet — and watches them fail to see it.

**Examples**:
- The audience knows the husband is the killer in ch.4 (subtext); the prosecutor doesn't until ch.10
- The audience knows the city will fall in ch.2 (because we showed the future); the protagonist works to prevent it

The fold creates a special engagement — the audience leans forward because they're "ahead" of the characters.

**Caution**: dramatic irony only works if the characters are competent and serious. Stupid characters break the fold.

---

## 5. The information gap (curiosity hook)

The audience is given enough to want more, but not enough to predict.

**Patterns**:
- Show effect first, withhold cause: "the doctor would not look at the others. He had signed the death certificate in January. He had not, that day, examined the throat." — what did he do?
- Name a rule, then show its violation: "no court would hear a dead girl as a witness" + later, the dead girl's words enter the record
- Open a quote without context: "It was no dream", she said — what wasn't a dream?

The gap should close within 1–3 chapters. Gaps held too long (>5 chapters) become frustrating, not engaging.

---

## 6. The braided POV

Two POVs alternate, creating contrast and complementarity.

**Examples**:
- Mother (emotional / spectral register) + Prosecutor (legal / forensic register)
- Detective (logic / procedure) + Victim's diary (emotion / interiority)
- Inventor (vision / obsession) + Investor (skepticism / numbers)

Braid rules:
- Each POV has its own voice register
- Transitions are clean (paragraph break, scene break)
- The braid converges in ch.16 reveal — both POVs see the truth at the same moment

---

## 7. The refused corroboration

When the audience expects confirmation, withhold it. Let the audience hold ambiguity.

**Example**: a ghost visits → "the candle had not guttered, and the dog under the porch had not stirred." The narrator REFUSES to corroborate the supernatural.

The technique:
- List the omens that WOULD confirm
- Note their absence
- Let the audience decide

This is essential for ambiguous-resolution stories. The narrator never asserts; the reader decides.

---

## 8. The ironic reversal (closing technique)

The end inverts a setup expectation:
- The lynch mob the LAW outraces
- The miser whose fortune was buried in his enemy's cellar all along
- The "witch" who saved her village by burning

The reversal works because Acts I–III set up the expectation. The reversal in Act IV pays off the dramatic ledger.

---

## 9. The historical-marker coda

Ground the story in present-day reality. Often a roadside marker, a museum plaque, a graveyard, an archive.

**Pattern**:
> On US-60, near Sam Black Church, a roadside marker stands today. Its text reads, in part: *Only known case in which testimony from ghost helped convict a murderer.* The marker is, in its way, wrong.

The coda:
- Reminds the audience this happened
- Provides factual closure (or corrects a misconception)
- Links to discoverable physical evidence

Works especially well for true-crime / history. Less natural for fiction.

## 10. Review dimensions (W3 self-review + external review)

The primary review lens is **Engagement (curiosity + expectation = engagement)**. The W3 reviewer reads the script as a viewer would — paragraph by paragraph — and asks: "does this make me want to keep reading?"

### Engagement (primary — overrides craft on conflict)

**E0. Curiosity maintenance** — does each scene/paragraph keep the audience asking "what happens next?" or "what does this mean?" If 3+ consecutive paragraphs answer no question and pose no new one, that's a drop-off zone.

**E1. Expectation accumulation** — does each scene plant or stoke a specific anticipation that earns a later payoff? Vague atmospheric build-up that pays nothing later = wasted space.

**E2. Engagement curve match** — does the prose-level pacing track the synopsis's chapter-by-chapter engagement curve? Local peaks land where the synopsis says they should.

**E3. No drop-off zones** — list explicitly: any beat (paragraph, sentence, dialogue exchange) where a viewer would mentally check out. These get prose-level rewrites, not editorial trims.

### Craft dimensions (subordinate — pass these without engagement passing = still revise)

C1. **Hook strength** — does the first 60s earn the next 9 minutes?
C2. **Reveal landing** — does ch.16 feel inevitable AND surprising?
C3. **Ambiguity / emotional core preserved** — is the central thematic question still alive at the final frame?
C4. **Period / setting voice** — vocabulary accuracy, anachronism count
C5. **Tension oscillation** — does it pulse rather than rise monotonically?
C6. **Pacing across acts** — does each act earn its word budget?
C7. **Foreshadow setup → payoff** — all seeds traceable in prose?
C8. **Word-budget compliance** — within the band agreed in the synopsis
C9. **Voice integrity** — does the narrator (and any character with direct dialogue) maintain register?
C10. **Adherence to the per-episode supplement** — does the script follow the supplement's tone, vocabulary, and avoid list?

### Pass criteria

- Engagement (E0–E3): **all four MUST pass**. Even one failure = revise (drop-off zones get rewritten).
- Craft (C1–C10): average ≥ 9.5 = pass; below 9.5 = revise.

If craft scores high but engagement fails: still revise. Craft never compensates for engagement collapse.

## 11. The supplement override

The per-episode `_meta_supplement.md` may specify:
- "Use this technique" or "avoid this technique" for the current episode
- "Use this signature phrase / motif"
- "The benchmark scripts use technique X; we should adapt it as Y"

Supplement instructions take precedence over this universal toolkit when they conflict. Document conflicts in the W3 hand-off.
