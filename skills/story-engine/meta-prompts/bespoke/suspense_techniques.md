# Suspense Techniques — Bespoke Genre (Universal)

Genre-neutral suspense toolkit for W2 (synopsis) and W3 (writing). Suspense ≠ fear; suspense is **uncertainty about an outcome the audience cares about**. The toolkit is universal — applies to mystery, biography, romance (will-they-or-won't-they), business (will it succeed?), comedy (will the lie be exposed?), etc.

---

## 1. Curiosity vs. expectation (the dual-engine)

Two engines drive engagement:

| Engine | Mechanism | Audience state |
|---|---|---|
| **Curiosity** | Information withheld → audience wants to know | "What happened?" |
| **Expectation** | Outcome promised → audience wants to see it land | "How will this end?" |

Both must be alive simultaneously through Acts I–III. If only curiosity → audience may stop caring once they see the answer. If only expectation → audience can predict the ending and disengage.

**Rule of thumb**: introduce both within the first 3 chapters. Re-stoke each at every act break.

---

## 2. The open loop

State a question or claim early; resolve it late.

**Examples**:
- "It was no dream", she said. (What wasn't a dream? Resolved at ch.16 cross-examination.)
- "He had not, that day, examined the throat." (Why didn't he? Resolved at ch.8 autopsy.)
- "She would not retract." (What did she insist on? Resolved across the script.)

Loop rules:
- Each loop has a clear OPEN moment and a clear CLOSE moment
- 2–4 loops per script ideal; >5 = audience tracking fatigue
- Distribute loops across acts; avoid clustering in Act I

---

## 3. The information asymmetry

The audience knows something a character doesn't (or vice versa).

| Direction | Effect | Use case |
|---|---|---|
| **Audience > character** | Dramatic irony — audience leans forward to see character realize | Mystery (audience suspects before detective) |
| **Character > audience** | Audience curiosity — wants to learn what character knows | Investigation reveal |
| **Neither** | Genuine mystery — both audience and characters at the same level | True ensemble whodunit |

Mix these deliberately. The classic mystery pattern: audience starts with NEITHER; in Act II they suspect (audience > character drift); in Act III the character catches up; ch.16 they converge.

---

## 4. The false resolution

Set up a finale-feeling moment in Act III that turns out to NOT be the actual climax.

**Pattern**:
- Ch.14: "the case is thin, the defense is winning"
- Ch.15: "the cross-examination is going badly for the prosecution"
- Ch.16: "Then he made his mistake" — ACTUAL climax

The false resolution:
- Triggers fight-or-flight in the audience (this is going wrong!)
- Creates the surprise space for the real reveal
- Earns the catharsis when the real reveal lands

---

## 5. The reversal pattern (paired with #4)

The false resolution is followed by an inversion — what looked like victory becomes defeat (or vice versa). The seeds for the inversion are planted in Acts I–II; the inversion fires in ch.16.

**Pattern checks**:
- Did Acts I–II plant the inversion seeds? (Yes = earned. No = ass-pulled.)
- Does the inversion happen via the antagonist's own action? (Strongest form: the gambit backfires.)
- Does the audience feel "I should have seen that coming" + "I didn't see that coming"?

The latter paradox (inevitable + surprising) is the highest-quality reveal.

---

## 6. The ticking clock (when applicable)

A deadline forces decisions. Works in any genre with a stakes-in-time element:

- Murder trial dates (June 22 trial → finite procedural window)
- Pregnancy / illness countdown (biological clock)
- Investor deadline (fundraising round closes)
- Mob arrival (lynching attempt — the law beats the mob to it)

Clock rules:
- The clock must be VISIBLE in Acts I–II (we know the deadline)
- The clock must MATTER in Act III (the deadline reshapes choices)
- The clock should NOT just expire — it should be beaten, missed, or transformed

If the topic has no natural clock, don't fake one. Some stories don't need it.

---

## 7. The strategic reveal cadence

Reveals are NOT distributed evenly. Cluster them:

| Act | Reveal density | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| I (1–5) | Low (1–2 small) | Establish stakes, plant seeds |
| II (6–12) | Medium (3–4 mid) | Each new fact tightens the noose; raise tension |
| III (13–17) | High → Maximum | Cascade in ch.16; final small reveal at 17 |
| IV (18–20) | Low (1 thematic) | Coda truth — what does it mean? |

If your synopsis has 10+ reveals before ch.10, you're front-loading. Restructure.

---

## 8. The weighted absence (refused corroboration)

The audience expects a sign; you withhold it. The withholding becomes meaningful.

**Examples**:
- The candle that should have guttered (didn't → ambiguity preserved)
- The dog that should have barked (didn't → also ambiguity)
- The tell that the antagonist should have given (he didn't → unsettling)

This is the "Sherlock Holmes silver-blaze" technique. The dog that did NOT bark is louder than one that did.

---

## 9. The procedural strangeness

When real-world rules behave oddly, surface it as suspense:

**Example**: "no American court will hear a dead girl as a witness" → and yet her testimony entered the record. The procedural strangeness makes the audience lean in.

Use when:
- The story has a real legal / institutional / technological constraint
- The reveal involves the constraint being technically violated or sidestepped

This is high-quality suspense in true-crime, courtroom drama, business story, sci-fi (technical-rule violations).

---

## 10. The held quiet (closing technique)

After the climax, the script can do one of:

| Pattern | When to use |
|---|---|
| **Held quiet** | Ambiguous resolution; thematic close on a still image | History, true-crime, biography |
| **Earned cathartic moment** | Triumph or tragedy clearly landed | Sports, action, war story |
| **Cliffhanger** | Series episode pointing to next | Ongoing series only |

For Bespoke genre default: HELD QUIET. The audience leaves with the image, not the answer.

## 11. The supplement contract

Suspense techniques in the per-episode `_meta_supplement.md` (derived from the user's reference scripts) take precedence when they conflict with this universal toolkit. The supplement may say "the reference scripts use a held-quiet close in their genre, so we follow that" — even if a different close might seem more universally appropriate.

Read both files; supplement wins on conflicts; document conflicts in the synopsis hand-off.
